CHAPTER 12: MACROECONOMICS & GLOBAL MARKETS
Covering: GDP & Economic Growth · RBI & Interest Rates · Federal Reserve & Global Liquidity · Currency Markets · Bond Markets · Oil Prices & India · Trade Deficits · Geopolitics & Markets · Recession Indicators · Global Capital Flows

The Hook
June 2022. US Federal Reserve announced a 75 basis point rate hike — the largest single hike since 1994. The next morning, Nifty 50 fell 1,500 points. Not because Indian companies did anything wrong. Not because Indian GDP changed. Because a committee of 12 people in Washington DC decided to raise the US interest rate.
FII (Foreign Institutional Investor) money, which had poured ₹2.7 lakh crore into Indian markets in FY21, pulled out ₹1.4 lakh crore in FY22 as US rates rose. They moved money from "emerging market risk" to "US risk-free treasury" because the US was now paying 5% with zero credit risk.
Your Nifty portfolio, your SIP, your Infosys shares — they are all connected to decisions made in Washington, Frankfurt, Tokyo, and Beijing. Ignoring macroeconomics while investing in equity is like sailing without understanding weather patterns.
The investors who understood in early 2022 that Fed rate hikes were coming shifted toward defensive sectors, reduced duration in debt portfolios, and held higher cash. They lost significantly less.
Can you connect the next RBI policy decision to your portfolio allocation — or does macroeconomics feel irrelevant to your investments?

The Concept
GDP (Gross Domestic Product) measures total economic output. India's GDP growth above 7% = strong earnings growth environment for corporates = equity bullish. Below 5% = slowdown risk = defensive positioning.
Interest Rates and RBI: When RBI raises repo rate: borrowing costs rise, consumer demand falls, corporate margins compress, equity P/E multiples contract. When RBI cuts: opposite. Rate cycles create sector rotation opportunities.
Bond Market Rule: When interest rates rise, bond prices fall (inverse relationship). A 10-year government bond with 6.5% coupon becomes less attractive when new bonds offer 7% — so its price falls to equalise yield.

Bond Price = Coupon ÷ (1 + Interest Rate)^Years

Federal Reserve and Global Liquidity: US dollar is the world's reserve currency. When Fed hikes, dollar strengthens. Strong dollar pulls capital from emerging markets (EMs) like India, China, Brazil. Weak dollar pushes capital into EMs for higher returns. India's equity market is partially driven by global dollar liquidity cycles.
Currency Markets — INR Dynamics:

RBI keeps INR managed float — intervenes when rupee depreciates too fast
Weaker INR: good for IT exporters (Infosys, TCS), bad for oil importers (BPCL), bad for companies with USD debt
Key FX formula: If INR goes from 82 to 86 per USD, an IT company billing $100 million gets ₹860 crore instead of ₹820 crore — a 4.9% revenue boost without a single new client.

Oil Prices and India:
India imports ~85% of its crude oil. At $80/barrel, India's annual oil bill = approximately $110 billion. At $100/barrel, it rises to $140 billion — $30 billion more, which means higher fiscal deficit, higher inflation, weaker INR.

Every $10 crude rise = approximately 0.3–0.4% higher inflation
Sectors hit: aviation (IndiGo, Air India), paint companies (Asian Paints — crude-based raw materials), tyres (MRF)
Sectors benefited: ONGC, Oil India, Cairn (upstream producers)

Trade Deficit: India's imports exceed exports — creating a structural current account deficit. High deficit = INR depreciation pressure. India's gold imports alone add $35–40 billion to trade deficit annually.
Geopolitics and Markets: Russia-Ukraine war (2022) caused global wheat and energy price spikes. US-China semiconductor war created PLI opportunity for India. Middle East tensions affect oil prices immediately. Geopolitical events create temporary dislocation — buy quality companies during geopolitical-driven crashes.
Recession Indicators — Leading Signals:

Inverted yield curve (2-year bond yields above 10-year) — historically precedes US recession by 12–18 months
PMI falling below 50 for 2+ consecutive months
Consumer confidence index declining 3+ months
Housing starts falling sharply (US) or auto sales falling (India)

Global Capital Flows: FII and FPI (Foreign Portfolio Investment) data on NSDL/NSE shows daily foreign buying/selling. Sustained 4-week FII buying despite domestic weakness = smart global money entering = bullish signal.

The Gita Connection
Sanskrit Shloka:

यदा यदा हि धर्मस्य ग्लानिर्भवति भारत।
अभ्युत्थानमधर्मस्य तदात्मानं सृजाम्यहम्॥

Chapter & Verse: Bhagavad Gita 4.7
Transliteration: Yada yada hi dharmasya glanir bhavati Bharata, Abhyutthanam adharmasya tadatmanam srijamyaham.

Meaning: "Whenever and wherever there is a decline in religious practice, O descendant of Bharata, and a predominant rise of irreligion—at that time I descend Myself." 

Financial Application: The global economic order periodically breaks down — 2008 financial crisis, 2020 COVID, 1997 Asian crisis, 1991 India balance of payments crisis. Each time the economic dharma declines, the conditions for its restoration manifest. RBI rate cuts follow inflation. US stimulus follows recession. China reopening follows lockdowns. The macroeconomic investor who understands that disorder always precedes order — and positions for the restoration before headlines confirm it — captures the steepest part of the recovery. The Nifty bottom of 7,610 in March 2020 was the maximum "adharma" moment. The subsequent 130% recovery was the dharma restoration. Recognise the cycle. Act on the restoration.

Real Indian Case Study
India's 1991 Balance of Payments Crisis
By June 1991, India had foreign exchange reserves of only $1.2 billion — barely 15 days of import cover. The country was two weeks from defaulting on international debt obligations. Gold was physically airlifted to London as collateral for emergency IMF loans.
The macro indicators that were visible:

Fiscal deficit: 8.4% of GDP (unsustainable)
Current account deficit: ballooning due to Gulf war oil price spike
Foreign reserves: declining for 18 months before the crisis
Inflation: 13% — well above RBI's capacity to manage without structural reform

The political response: Finance Minister Manmohan Singh's July 1991 budget devalued the rupee by 18%, began liberalisation (ended License Raj), reduced import duties, opened FDI routes.
The market and economic outcome:

Nifty: from 1,000 (1991) to 6,000 (1999) — 6x in 8 years
Infosys founded 1981, listed 1993, went on to 200x returns
Foreign direct investment: negligible in 1990, $80+ billion annually by 2023

The macro lesson: India's greatest wealth creation opportunity was created at its worst macroeconomic moment. The investors who understood that structural reform after crisis = multi-decade growth captured it. Those focused only on the crisis (fiscal deficit, IMF loans, rupee devaluation) were too afraid to invest.

The Practical Playbook
Rule 1 — Monitor RBI monetary policy statement (MPC meeting, 6 times yearly) and act on it.
Rate hike cycle → reduce long-duration debt exposure, favour short-duration. Rate cut cycle → add long-duration debt, increase equity allocation. The direction matters, not the exact number.
Rule 2 — Track INR/USD weekly for IT and oil-linked stock positioning.
INR depreciating 5%+ in 3 months: add IT stocks, reduce aviation exposure. INR appreciating: the opposite. This is currency alpha that requires no forex trading — just equity reallocation.
Rule 3 — Follow FII monthly data on NSE/NSDL and track 4-week cumulative flow.
Four consecutive weeks of FII buying in Indian equity despite INR weakness = global capital building India position = medium-term bullish signal. Historically reliable entry indicator.
Rule 4 — Check Brent crude price weekly if you hold aviation, paint, or tyre stocks.
These sectors' margins are directly crude-linked. Crude above $90/barrel for 2+ months: consider reducing aviation exposure. Below $70: consider increasing.
Rule 5 — Study US Fed dot plot and forward guidance before RBI's next meeting.
RBI rarely diverges significantly from Fed policy direction due to capital flow dynamics. Fed hawkish = RBI likely hawkish. Knowing Fed's signal 4–6 weeks before RBI meeting gives portfolio repositioning lead time.

Common Mistakes
Mistake 1 — Ignoring global macro for "fundamentally strong" Indian stocks.
What they do: hold a quality Indian IT stock through a Fed rate hike cycle because "fundamentals are intact." Why it feels right: the company's business genuinely is strong. Why it destroys wealth: even strong businesses see P/E multiple compression during rate hike cycles — the stock falls 30% not because business failed but because global capital repriced the risk.
Mistake 2 — Treating INR depreciation as uniformly bad.
What they do: panic when INR falls from 82 to 86 and move to gold or FD. Why it feels right: weaker currency feels dangerous. Why it destroys wealth: INR depreciation is a systematic tailwind for IT, pharmaceutical export, and textile stocks — being in these during INR weakness creates substantial alpha.
Mistake 3 — Buying during geopolitical events expecting "it will recover fast."
What they do: buy heavily during geopolitical shocks (Russia-Ukraine, Middle East tensions) because they expect quick recovery. Why it feels right: geopolitical events historically resolve. Why it destroys wealth: geopolitical events with commodity price implications (oil, wheat) have 12–18 month economic lag effects — the economic damage outlasts the news cycle.

Chapter Summary

India's equity market is connected to global capital flows, US Fed decisions, and oil prices — macro cannot be ignored.
RBI's repo rate changes compress or expand equity P/E multiples — rate cycles must inform allocation decisions.
Bond prices and interest rates move inversely — rising rates hurt long-duration bond holders significantly.
INR depreciation is not uniformly negative: IT and pharma exporters benefit directly and measurably.
Oil at $10 higher means 0.3–0.4% additional inflation and $30 billion more in India's import bill.
The 1991 BOP crisis proved India's greatest investment opportunity followed its worst macroeconomic crisis.
Fed dot plot and forward guidance provide 4–6 weeks of advance signal before RBI policy response.
Global capital flow data (FII monthly on NSDL) is a leading indicator available free to every investor.


Quiz Questions
Q1: When the US Federal Reserve raises interest rates significantly, what typically happens to Indian equity markets?

A) Nifty rises as Indian companies benefit
B) FII capital flows to US risk-free assets; Nifty faces selling pressure
C) RBI immediately cuts rates to compensate
D) No effect since Indian equity is domestically driven
Correct: B
Explanation: Higher US rates make risk-free US treasury yields competitive vs EM equity. FIIs repatriate capital from India to capture higher US yields without EM risk — causing INR depreciation and Nifty selling pressure.

Q2: INR depreciating from ₹82 to ₹86 per USD most directly benefits:

A) Aviation companies importing aircraft
B) IT companies earning USD revenue and paying INR costs
C) FMCG companies with domestic sales only
D) Companies with large USD-denominated debt
Correct: B
Explanation: IT companies bill in USD. When USD strengthens, the same billing converts to more rupees — directly improving revenue and margins without operational change. This is systematic currency alpha for IT exporters.

Q3: India's 1991 BOP crisis was resolved primarily through:

A) RBI printing more rupees
B) IMF emergency bailout alone
C) Structural liberalisation — devaluation, opening FDI, reducing License Raj
D) Increasing import duties to protect reserves
Correct: C
Explanation: Manmohan Singh's budget began liberalisation reforms that changed India's economic architecture. The short-term crisis tools (IMF loan, devaluation) were complemented by structural reforms that created 30+ years of growth trajectory.

Q4: An inverted yield curve (2-year bonds yielding more than 10-year) indicates:

A) Short-term inflation is high
B) RBI is reducing overnight rates
C) Markets expect economic slowdown/recession ahead — historically a leading recession indicator
D) Government is issuing more 2-year bonds than 10-year
Correct: C
Explanation: Normally, longer maturity bonds yield more (risk premium). When short-term yields exceed long-term, it signals markets expect rates will need to fall (due to recession) in future. Inverted US yield curve preceded every US recession in the past 50 years.

Q5: Every $10 per barrel crude oil price increase affects India by:

A) Adding approximately ₹5,000 crore to government revenues
B) Adding approximately $30 billion to India's import bill and 0.3–0.4% to inflation
C) Increasing Nifty Pharma index by 5%
D) Strengthening INR due to oil company profits
Correct: B
Explanation: India imports ~85% of crude. $10 higher on ~3 billion barrels annual import = ~$30 billion additional outflow. This worsens current account deficit, pressures INR, and flows through to consumer price inflation in fuel, transport, and manufactured goods.
