CHAPTER 6: FUNDAMENTAL ANALYSIS
Covering: What Makes a Business Valuable · Balance Sheets · P&L Statements · Cash Flow · Revenue/Profit/Margins · P/E Ratio · ROE/ROCE · Debt Analysis · Competitive Moat · Finding Long-Term Compounders

The Hook
In 2003, a ₹1 lakh investment in Page Industries (maker of Jockey innerwear in India) would have become ₹6.5 crore by 2018. In the same period, hundreds of other companies on NSE delivered negative or zero returns.
The difference was not luck. Page Industries had every quality metric visible in their annual report: consistent 30%+ ROE, expanding margins, zero debt, category dominance, growing middle-class tailwind. All public information. All readable by anyone with a BSE account and 3 hours of time.
The people who bought Page Industries at ₹400 in 2003 and held to ₹25,000 in 2018 were not financial geniuses. They read the balance sheet. They understood what made the business structurally superior. They trusted the numbers over the noise.
Fundamental analysis is simply the discipline of reading a business's financial story and deciding if you want to own a piece of it at today's price.
Every listed company publishes their financial statements quarterly. The information is free. The discipline to read it is rare. That rarity is your edge.
Do you know how to read the financial story of the company you own?

The Concept
A business has value because it generates cash. Everything in fundamental analysis traces back to this one principle.
Balance Sheet: Snapshot of what a company owns (assets) and owes (liabilities) on a given date.

Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity
Key ratios: Debt-to-Equity (lower is safer), Current Ratio (current assets ÷ current liabilities, above 1.5 is healthy)

P&L Statement: Shows revenue, expenses, and profit over a period.

Revenue → Gross Profit → EBITDA → PAT (Profit After Tax)
Gross Margin = (Revenue − Cost of Goods) ÷ Revenue
High gross margins (40%+) indicate pricing power — companies like Asian Paints, HUL, Infosys maintain this.

Cash Flow Statement: The most honest financial document. Shows actual cash generated vs accounting profit.

Free Cash Flow (FCF) = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditure

Worked Example — Page Industries FY2018:

Revenue: ₹2,380 crore
PAT: ₹300 crore
Free Cash Flow: ₹245 crore
Debt: ₹0
ROE: 48%
P/E ratio at ₹25,000/share: approximately 55x

High P/E justified by: zero debt, 48% ROE, category monopoly, consistent 25%+ earnings growth for 10 years.
P/E Ratio = Market Price ÷ Earnings Per Share. It tells you how many rupees you pay for every ₹1 of annual earnings.

P/E of 15: paying ₹15 for ₹1 of annual earnings
Sector average P/E matters: FMCG trades at 40-50x, Banking at 10-15x, IT at 20-30x

ROE (Return on Equity) = Net Profit ÷ Shareholders' Equity. Measures how efficiently a company uses shareholder money. Sustained ROE above 20% indicates a quality business.
ROCE (Return on Capital Employed) = EBIT ÷ Capital Employed. Includes debt in the denominator — better measure for capital-intensive industries. ROCE above 15% is the baseline for quality.
Competitive Moat is the sustainable advantage that prevents competitors from replicating success:

Brand moat: HUL, Asian Paints — consumers trust the brand, pay premium
Cost moat: Tata Steel — scale advantages reduce per-unit cost
Network moat: NSE — more traders use NSE because more traders use NSE
Switching moat: TCS — changing enterprise software vendor costs clients crores
Regulatory moat: HDFC Bank, SBI — banking licences are government-controlled

Finding long-term compounders: Look for: ROE 20%+ for 5+ years, debt-to-equity below 0.5, consistent revenue growth 15%+, expanding or stable gross margins, identifiable moat, promoter holding above 40%.

The Gita Connection
Sanskrit Shloka:

ज्ञानं ते ऽहं सविज्ञानमिदं वक्ष्याम्यशेषतः।
यज्ज्ञात्वा नेह भूयोऽन्यज्ज्ञातव्यमवशिष्यते॥

Chapter & Verse: Bhagavad Gita 7.2
Transliteration: Jnanam te aham savijnanam idam vakshyamyasheshatah, Yajjnatva neha bhuyo anyaj jnatavyamavashishyate.
Word-by-word meaning:

Meaning: "I will declare to you in full this knowledge combined with realization, after knowing which nothing else remains to be known in this world."

Financial Application: Krishna promises complete knowledge — both theoretical and practical (savijnanam). This is exactly what fundamental analysis offers: the theoretical framework (ratios, formulas) plus the practical wisdom (which numbers matter, in which context, for which industry). Knowing only P/E ratio is half-knowledge. Knowing that FMCG P/E of 45 is justified by brand moat and pricing power but banking P/E of 45 is dangerously overvalued — that is savijnanam. Study both theory and context. When you have that complete picture, you will not be confused by noise.

Real Indian Case Study
Yes Bank — From ₹400 to ₹5 (2018–2020)
Yes Bank peaked at ₹404 in August 2018. By March 2020, it touched ₹5 — a 98.7% collapse. Thousands of retail investors, trusting the "premium private bank" brand, held all the way down.
The fundamental red flags were visible:

Gross NPA (Non-Performing Assets) rising from 0.59% (FY16) to 3.22% (FY19) — loan quality deteriorating
Divergence in NPA reporting (RBI found Yes Bank underreporting NPAs by ₹3,277 crore in FY17 audit)
Promoter pledging increasing — Rana Kapoor pledged significant stake, signal of financial stress
Loan book growing 30%+ annually with concentrated exposure to stressed sectors (IL&FS, DHFL)
ROA declining consistently while banks presented it as "growing business"

What fundamental analysis would have done:

NPA divergence in 2017 RBI audit = immediate exit signal
Promoter pledging above 30% = yellow flag, above 50% = red flag
Loan book growing faster than industry while NPA rising = structural risk

The investors who read these quarterly reports and exited before 2019 preserved capital. Those who trusted the brand and the dividend record lost 98.7%.
The lesson: The stock market is not a brand loyalty programme. Read the financials.

The Practical Playbook
Rule 1 — Check ROE, ROCE, and Debt-to-Equity before buying any stock.
Minimum thresholds: ROE 15%+, ROCE 12%+, Debt/Equity below 1.0 for non-financial companies. If any of these fail, require extraordinary justification or skip.
Rule 2 — Read the Cash Flow Statement, not just PAT.
If PAT is growing but Operating Cash Flow is declining, the company is either aggressive in accounting or capital is being consumed without returns. Free Cash Flow positive for 3+ consecutive years is a green flag.
Rule 3 — Check promoter pledging quarterly.
Promoter pledging above 30%: yellow flag (investigate why). Above 50%: consider exit. Pledged shares can be sold by lenders in distress, creating sudden stock collapse.
Rule 4 — Compare company metrics to sector peers, not absolute numbers.
A P/E of 30 is cheap for FMCG and expensive for a PSU bank. Use sector context: compare Infosys P/E to TCS, Wipro — not to ITC.
Rule 5 — Hold minimum 3 years post purchase — check thesis annually, not quarterly.
Quarterly earnings noise distracts from 3–5 year business trajectories. Check: is the moat intact? Is ROE stable? Is management executing the stated plan? Annual review is sufficient.

Common Mistakes
Mistake 1 — Buying stocks based on brand familiarity.
What they do: buy Zomato, Paytm, Ola because they use the apps daily. Why it feels right: "I understand this business because I use it." Why it destroys wealth: consumer familiarity ≠ business quality. Paytm listed at ₹2,150, fell to ₹440. The app being popular said nothing about profitability timeline.
Mistake 2 — Ignoring NPA and asset quality for banking stocks.
What they do: buy banking stocks based on branch count and brand advertising. Why it feels right: visible, trusted institutions seem safe. Why it destroys wealth: a bank's value is entirely in its loan book quality — invisible until it is not.
Mistake 3 — Treating high dividends as quality signal.
What they do: buy companies paying 5–7% dividend yield as "safe income." Why it feels right: regular cash payout seems like a stable business indicator. Why it destroys wealth: high dividend yield can indicate a company returning capital because it cannot reinvest profitably — often a sign of declining business quality.

Chapter Summary

A business has value only because it generates real cash — trace every metric back to this principle.
Balance sheet tells you what company owns and owes; P&L shows profitability; cash flow shows truth.
ROE above 20% sustained over 5+ years is the primary identifier of quality businesses.
Free Cash Flow is more reliable than PAT — companies can manipulate accounting profit but not cash.
P/E ratio must be compared within industry context; cross-industry P/E comparison is meaningless.
Competitive moat (brand, cost, network, switching, regulatory) determines whether earnings will sustain.
Promoter pledging above 30% is a structural risk signal requiring investigation.
Yes Bank's collapse was visible in quarterly reports three years before the price crashed 98%.


Quiz Questions
Q1: Free Cash Flow is calculated as:

A) Revenue minus operating expenses
B) Operating Cash Flow minus Capital Expenditure
C) Net Profit minus taxes
D) EBITDA minus depreciation
Correct: B
Explanation: FCF = Operating Cash Flow − Capex. It shows cash actually generated after maintaining and growing the business — the purest measure of business value creation.

Q2: Yes Bank's fundamental red flag in 2017 was:

A) Their ATM network was insufficient
B) RBI found NPA underreporting by ₹3,277 crore in audit
C) Their P/E ratio exceeded 30
D) Management resigned suddenly
Correct: B
Explanation: NPA divergence in RBI's audit meant Yes Bank was hiding loan stress. Any investor reading the 2017 annual report or RBI circular had this information — most did not look.

Q3: ROCE is preferred over ROE for capital-intensive industries because:

A) ROCE is easier to calculate
B) ROCE includes debt in its denominator, giving a fuller picture of capital efficiency
C) SEBI mandates ROCE reporting
D) ROE does not account for profitability
Correct: B
Explanation: Capital-intensive companies (steel, infrastructure, manufacturing) carry significant debt. ROE only measures equity return; ROCE measures total capital deployed efficiency — more complete picture.

Q4: A company with rising PAT but declining Operating Cash Flow indicates:

A) A healthy, growing business
B) Possible aggressive accounting or deteriorating working capital management
C) Tax planning efficiency
D) SEBI mandated accounting changes
Correct: B
Explanation: PAT can be inflated through accounting choices (revenue recognition timing, depreciation methods). Cash flow cannot be similarly manipulated — divergence signals investigation needed.

Q5: Page Industries' justification for 55x P/E in 2018 was:

A) Government contract guarantees
B) Zero debt, 48% ROE, category monopoly, and 25%+ consistent earnings growth
C) Promoter was a famous industrialist
D) NSE classified it as a blue-chip stock
Correct: B
Explanation: High P/E is justified by: zero debt (no financial risk), exceptional ROE (capital efficiency), moat (Jockey brand dominance), and consistent growth (earnings compounding). All four must be present.
