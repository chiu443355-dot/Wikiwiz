CHAPTER 10: PORTFOLIO CONSTRUCTION
Covering: Asset Allocation · Equity/Debt/Gold Mix · Diversification Strategy · Age-Based Design · Rebalancing · Risk-Based Allocation · Emergency Funds · Goal-Based Investing · Tax-Efficient Investing · Complete Portfolio

The Hook
Three investors in 2020. All had ₹20 lakh to invest.
Investor A: 100% equity. Nifty crashed 38%. Portfolio hit ₹12.4 lakh. Panic-sold in March. Missed recovery. Bought back at 11,000. Underperformed by ₹8 lakh.
Investor B: 100% FD. Safe from crash. But 6% inflation on 5% FD over 3 years = real loss of ₹1.8 lakh in purchasing power.
Investor C: 60% equity (Nifty index funds), 25% debt (short-duration bond funds), 15% gold (SGBs). When equity fell 38%, gold rose 24%, debt was stable. Total portfolio fell only 17%. She did not panic. She rebalanced — sold some gold and debt to buy more equity at the bottom. By December 2021, her portfolio was at ₹35 lakh. Investor A was at ₹26 lakh. Investor B was at ₹23.5 lakh.
Portfolio construction is not about picking the best asset. It is about combining assets so the portfolio's behavior allows you to stay invested through inevitable crises.
The architecture matters more than the individual bricks.
Have you designed your portfolio — or is it a random collection of products you bought at different times for different reasons?

The Concept
Asset Allocation is the single biggest determinant of portfolio returns and risk. Studies by Brinson, Hood, and Beebower showed 91.5% of portfolio variability is explained by asset allocation — not stock selection or market timing.

Age-Based Allocation Rule (100 minus age):

Age 25: 75% equity, 15% debt, 10% gold
Age 40: 60% equity, 25% debt, 15% gold
Age 55: 40% equity, 40% debt, 20% gold
Age 65: 25% equity, 50% debt, 25% gold

This is a starting framework. Adjust based on risk tolerance, income stability, and financial goals.
Emergency Fund (Non-Negotiable Foundation):

Emergency Fund = 6 months of household expenses, in liquid mutual fund or sweep FD

Without this, any financial shock — job loss, medical emergency, car repair — forces selling investments at random times, including market bottoms.
Goal-Based Investing: Each financial goal gets its own portfolio with appropriate asset allocation and time horizon.

3-year goal (car, house down payment): 30% equity, 70% debt (capital preservation priority)
7-year goal (child's college): 60% equity, 30% debt, 10% gold
20-year goal (retirement): 80% equity, 10% debt, 10% gold

Worked Example — 30-year-old with ₹10 lakh to invest:

Emergency fund: ₹1.5 lakh in liquid fund (already separate)
Goal 1 — Car in 3 years: ₹2 lakh → 30% Nifty 50 + 70% short-duration debt fund
Goal 2 — Retirement in 30 years: ₹5 lakh → 80% Nifty 50 index + 10% SGB + 10% PPNPF
Goal 3 — Child education in 15 years: ₹2.5 lakh → 60% Nifty 50 + 20% mid-cap index + 20% debt

Rebalancing: If equity rises to 75% of target 60%, sell 15% equity and buy debt/gold. Annual rebalancing captures the "sell high, buy low" automatically.
Tax-Efficient Investing:

Equity LTCG (above ₹1 lakh): 10% — hold equity beyond 12 months
Debt LTCG (post-3 years): taxed at income slab — keep debt in EPF, PPF, NPS where tax-advantaged
ELSS funds: ₹1.5 lakh deduction under 80C, shortest lock-in among tax-saving options (3 years)
SGB: zero capital gains tax at maturity — strictly superior to gold ETF for 8-year hold

Complete portfolio for ₹10,000/month SIP capacity:

₹5,000 Nifty 50 index fund (core equity)
₹2,000 Mid-cap 150 index fund (growth equity)
₹2,000 Short-duration debt fund (stability)
₹1,000 Gold ETF or SGB (hedge)
Total: ₹10,000/month — covers all major asset classes, diversified, tax-aware.


The Gita Connection
Sanskrit Shloka:

तस्माद्यस्य महाबाहो निगृहीतानि सर्वशः।
इन्द्रियाणीन्द्रियार्थेभ्यस्तस्य प्रज्ञा प्रतिष्ठिता॥

Chapter & Verse: Bhagavad Gita 2.68
Transliteration: Tasmad yasya mahabaho nigrahitani sarvashah, Indriyani indriyarthebhyas tasya prajna pratishthita.
Word-by-word meaning:

Meaning: "Therefore, O mighty-armed Arjuna, one whose senses are completely restrained from their sense objects, his wisdom is firmly fixed (in transcendental consciousness).

Financial Application: The investor with a designed portfolio has their senses (emotions) controlled. When markets crash, they rebalance mechanically — not react emotionally. When gold spikes, they trim — not celebrate and buy more. Portfolio construction is the act of making your investment decisions in advance, when you are calm and rational, so that market volatility cannot override them with emotion. The "senses" in investing are FOMO, panic, greed, pride. A well-constructed portfolio with rebalancing rules removes the need for these senses to make decisions. Prajna — steady wisdom — is what the designed portfolio creates.

Real Indian Case Study
Franklin Templeton Fund Wind-Up — April 2020
In April 2020, Franklin Templeton India announced winding up of 6 debt mutual fund schemes with combined AUM of ₹25,215 crore. Investors could not redeem their money for months. Some schemes took 2+ years to fully return capital.
Why it happened:

Franklin had concentrated exposure in low-rated, illiquid corporate bonds (IL&FS, Vodafone Idea, Yes Bank paper)
COVID triggered mass redemptions — Franklin couldn't sell illiquid bonds fast enough to pay redeeming investors
Result: gate closure (redemptions halted) — investors trapped

Portfolio construction lessons:

Debt funds are not FD equivalents — credit risk is real
Never put more than 30% of liquid reserves in credit-risk debt funds
Emergency fund must be in sovereign-backed liquid funds (Overnight funds, Liquid funds with only government securities)
Diversification across fund houses — concentration in one AMC adds operational risk

What correct portfolio construction prevents: An investor with ₹25 lakh structured as — ₹5 lakh emergency (bank FD + overnight fund), ₹15 lakh equity (index funds), ₹5 lakh debt (short-duration only, AAA-rated) — would have had zero Franklin exposure and sailed through 2020.

The Practical Playbook
Rule 1 — Build emergency fund completely before any investment.
Six months of expenses in liquid fund or sweep FD. This is not part of your investment portfolio. It is a non-negotiable foundation. Without it, every portfolio decision is a crisis decision.
Rule 2 — Rebalance annually, always triggered by 5%+ deviation from target allocation.
If equity target is 60% and it reaches 67%, sell 7% equity and buy debt/gold. This is automatic "sell high, buy low" discipline.
Rule 3 — Never put debt investment in credit-risk funds without understanding the risk.
For capital preservation (emergency fund, short-term goals), use only overnight funds or liquid funds investing in government securities. Credit risk funds are for returns-enhancement only — treat them like equity in terms of risk categorisation.
Rule 4 — Use tax-advantaged accounts to their maximum before taxable accounts.
EPF: mandatory, leverage it. PPF: ₹1.5 lakh/year, 15-year lock-in, 7.1% tax-free compound — exceptional for debt component. NPS: additional ₹50,000 80CCD(1B) deduction. ELSS: ₹1.5 lakh 80C, 3-year lock-in, equity returns.
Rule 5 — Match investment horizon to asset class for every goal.
Money needed in less than 3 years: maximum 30% equity. Never put short-term money in volatile assets — markets take 2–4 years to recover from major corrections.

Common Mistakes
Mistake 1 — Treating portfolio as a collection, not a system.
What they do: buy funds, stocks, FDs, and insurance plans independently based on individual promotions or advice. Why it feels right: each individual product seems reasonable. Why it destroys wealth: no coherent allocation, high overlap between equity funds, no rebalancing plan, multiple products with contradictory objectives.
Mistake 2 — Over-diversifying into too many mutual funds.
What they do: hold 12–15 mutual funds to "diversify." Why it feels right: more funds seem safer. Why it destroys wealth: 10+ equity funds often hold largely overlapping Nifty 50 stocks. It creates tracking-error complexity without additional diversification benefit. 3–4 funds across categories are sufficient.
Mistake 3 — Not accounting for tax while comparing investments.
What they do: compare a 7.5% FD to a 7.5% debt mutual fund as equivalent. Why it feels right: same headline number. Why it destroys wealth: FD interest is taxed at income slab (30% = 5.25% post-tax). Debt fund held 3 years gets indexation benefit significantly reducing tax. Post-tax comparison is the only honest comparison.

Chapter Summary

Asset allocation explains 91.5% of portfolio returns variability — get this right before picking any product.
Emergency fund of 6 months' expenses is the non-negotiable foundation — build it first, always.
Age-based allocation (100 minus age in equity) is a simple starting framework, not a rigid rule.
Goal-based investing creates separate sub-portfolios with appropriate risk for each time horizon.
Annual rebalancing with 5%+ trigger automatically enforces "sell high, buy low" discipline.
Tax-advantaged accounts (EPF, PPF, NPS, ELSS) must be maximised before taxable investments.
Franklin Templeton wind-up proved: debt funds carry credit risk; sovereign-backed funds for emergency.
Three to four well-chosen funds across equity, debt, and gold cover 95% of portfolio needs efficiently.


Quiz Questions
Q1: What percentage of portfolio return variability is explained by asset allocation, per research?

A) 30%
B) 60%
C) 91.5%
D) 100%
Correct: C
Explanation: Brinson, Hood, and Beebower's landmark study found 91.5% of long-term return variability comes from asset allocation decisions, not market timing or individual security selection.

Q2: For a 3-year financial goal, the appropriate equity allocation is approximately:

A) 80%
B) 60%
C) 30%
D) 100%
Correct: C
Explanation: Markets can take 2–4 years to recover from major corrections. A 3-year goal cannot survive a 30–40% equity drawdown with no recovery time. 30% equity limits downside while allowing some growth above debt returns.

Q3: Franklin Templeton's 2020 fund wind-up was caused by:

A) RBI regulation change for mutual funds
B) Concentration in illiquid, low-rated bonds combined with COVID-triggered redemption surge
C) SEBI audit finding compliance failures
D) Franklin's parent company US bankruptcy
Correct: B
Explanation: The combination of illiquid bond portfolio and sudden mass redemptions created a mismatch — funds could not sell bonds fast enough to pay redeeming investors, forcing the gate closure.

Q4: Annual rebalancing is triggered when:

A) Market crashes more than 10%
B) Portfolio allocation deviates more than 5% from target
C) RBI changes repo rate
D) Calendar turns to January each year
Correct: B
Explanation: Rebalancing on deviation (not calendar) is more responsive to actual allocation drift. A bull market can push equity from 60% to 70% target in 6 months — waiting for December means taking higher risk unnecessarily.

Q5: Sovereign Gold Bonds are tax-advantaged over gold ETFs because:

A) SGBs have lower expense ratio
B) Capital gains on SGBs are tax-free at maturity (8 years); gold ETFs are taxed as debt
C) SGBs are exempt from wealth tax
D) SGBs do not count toward asset allocation calculations
Correct: B
Explanation: SGB maturity proceeds are completely exempt from capital gains tax. Gold ETFs held below 3 years are taxed at income slab; above 3 years at 20% with indexation. For 8-year hold, SGBs save 10–20% tax on the entire gain.
