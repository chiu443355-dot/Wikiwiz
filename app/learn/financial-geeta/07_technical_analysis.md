CHAPTER 7: TECHNICAL ANALYSIS
Covering: Charts · Candlestick Psychology · Support & Resistance · Trends · Volume · Moving Averages · RSI · Breakouts/Fakeouts · Risk Management · Building a Trading System

The Hook
Two traders. Both looking at the same Nifty chart in March 2020.
Trader A sees: "Market has crashed 38%. It might crash more. I should wait."
Trader B sees: "Price touched the 2016 support level at 7,500. Weekly RSI is at 22 — historically oversold. Volume on the crash days is declining. This looks like capitulation bottom. Risk: ₹200 below 7,300. Reward: ₹2,000 back to previous high."
Trader B bought Nifty futures at 7,610. By June 2020, Nifty crossed 10,000. ₹2,390 profit per lot from a ₹200 risk.
Same chart. Same data. Completely different outcomes. The difference is a framework for reading price and volume to make probabilistic decisions.
Technical analysis does not predict the future. It maps the probability landscape so you can position yourself where risk is lowest and reward potential is highest.
Most retail traders lose in technical analysis not because the tools fail — but because they use them without a system, without risk management, and without discipline.
Do you have a written trading system — or do you make decisions based on chart patterns you saw in a YouTube video 2 hours ago?

The Concept
Technical analysis operates on three assumptions: price discounts all information, prices move in trends, and history repeats because human psychology repeats.
Candlestick basics: Each candle shows Open, High, Low, Close for a time period. The body shows Open-Close range; wicks show High-Low range.

Bullish engulfing: Large green candle engulfs previous red — buyers overwhelmed sellers
Doji: Open ≈ Close — indecision, often precedes reversal
Hammer: Small body, long lower wick — selling was rejected, buyers stepped in

Support: Price level where buying repeatedly overcomes selling (price bounces up). Resistance: Price level where selling repeatedly overcomes buying (price bounces down). When price breaks above resistance, that level becomes new support. When it breaks below support, that level becomes new resistance.
Trends: Higher highs and higher lows = uptrend. Lower highs and lower lows = downtrend. Trade in the direction of the trend. "Trend is your friend until it ends."
Volume Analysis: Volume confirms price moves. Rising price + rising volume = strong bullish move. Rising price + falling volume = weak move, suspect rally. A breakout above resistance on 3x average volume is significantly more reliable than on 0.5x volume.
Moving Averages:

50 DMA (Daily Moving Average): Short-term trend indicator
200 DMA: Long-term trend indicator
Golden Cross: 50 DMA crosses above 200 DMA = bullish signal
Death Cross: 50 DMA crosses below 200 DMA = bearish signal

Nifty trading above its 200 DMA = broadly bullish environment. Below = bearish.
RSI (Relative Strength Index): Measures momentum on 0–100 scale.

RSI = 100 − [100 ÷ (1 + Average Gain ÷ Average Loss)]


Above 70: overbought (not necessarily a sell — in strong uptrends, RSI stays high)
Below 30: oversold (historically, strong buying opportunity in long-term uptrends)

Worked Example: Nifty Bank at 43,000. RSI = 28. Price at 3-year support. Stop loss at 42,000. Target at 46,500. Risk: ₹1,000. Reward: ₹3,500. Ratio: 3.5:1. Entry justified.
Breakouts: Price moving above resistance on high volume. Trade the breakout with stop below broken resistance. Fakeouts: False breakout that reverses — avoid by waiting for 2-day close above breakout level before entering.
Building a trading system: Define entry criteria, stop loss method, target method, position sizing rule, maximum daily loss, and review process. Write it. Follow it. Any trade that does not match your written criteria is not your system — it is your emotion.

Chapter & Verse: Bhagavad Gita 6.5
Transliteration: Uddharedatmana atmanam natmanam avasadayet, Atmaiva hyatmano bandhur atmaiva ripur atmanah.
Word-by-word meaning:

Meaning : "One should elevate oneself through one's own efforts, and should not degrade oneself. For, oneself is one's own friend, and oneself is also one's own enemy."

Financial Application: In trading, you are your own best friend and your own worst enemy. Your system is your higher self — your emotions are your lower self. When you follow your stop loss even when your "feeling" says the trade will reverse, you are elevating yourself. The technical system is just a tool; the discipline to follow it is the entire game.

Real Indian Case Study
Nifty 50 — The March 2020 Bottom and Recovery
Nifty peaked at 12,362 on January 20, 2020. By March 23, it was at 7,610 — 38.5% crash in 63 days. It was the fastest bear market in Indian history.
Technical signals at the 7,610 low (visible to any chart reader):

Price reached the 2016 breakout support level (7,500–7,600 zone, tested 4 years prior)
RSI on weekly chart: 22 — extreme oversold, historically reliable reversal signal
Volume pattern: highest volume day on March 23 (capitulation — all sellers exhausted)
Gap between current price and 200 DMA: 42% — historically extreme, mean reversion likely

Trading system response:

Entry: 7,650 (small initial position, confirming support hold)
Stop: 7,300 (below key support, risk ₹350)
Target 1: 9,000 (reward ₹1,350 — 3.86:1 ratio)
Target 2: 12,000 (full recovery, reward ₹4,350)

By August 2020, Nifty crossed 11,000. By January 2021, back to 14,000. By December 2021, at 17,500.
The trader without a system: Waited for "clarity" and re-entered at 10,000 — missing 31% of the move.
The system trader: Took defined risk at 7,650, followed targets, exited on schedule.
The lesson: technical analysis at market extremes is highly reliable — not because charts predict the future but because human behaviour at extremes is predictable.

The Practical Playbook
Rule 1 — Only trade in the direction of the 200 DMA.
If price is above 200 DMA: look for long (buy) setups only. Below 200 DMA: look for short setups or stay in cash. Fighting the 200 DMA trend is statistically negative expected value.
Rule 2 — Require volume confirmation for all breakout trades.
A breakout above resistance on volume less than 1.5x 20-day average volume is not a valid breakout. Wait for the confirmation candle.
Rule 3 — Use RSI below 30 on weekly charts as a shopping list signal, not immediate buy.
RSI below 30 on weekly says: "start looking for entry triggers." A confirming bullish candle pattern the following week is the actual entry signal. RSI alone is not a trading system.
Rule 4 — Pre-define daily maximum loss: stop trading at −2% of portfolio for the day.
If your ₹5 lakh trading account loses ₹10,000 in a day, stop. No revenge trading. No "one more trade." Close the screen.
Rule 5 — Paper trade any new technical system for 30 trades before risking real money.
Track entry, stop, target, actual outcome for 30 hypothetical trades. If system shows positive expectancy, go live. If not, refine. Never test a system with real money in real time.

Common Mistakes
Mistake 1 — Overloading charts with 6+ indicators.
What they do: add RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands, Stochastic, ATR, and Fibonacci simultaneously. Why it feels right: more data seems like more clarity. Why it destroys wealth: conflicting signals from multiple indicators create paralysis or arbitrary selection of whichever confirms the trade they already want to take.
Mistake 2 — Moving stop losses wider after trade entry.
What they do: set stop at ₹2,280, price drops to ₹2,290, they move stop to ₹2,200 "to give it more room." Why it feels right: feels like patience. Why it destroys wealth: systematically increases loss size and trains the brain to override risk management — the one skill that keeps traders alive.
Mistake 3 — Treating technical patterns as predictions.
What they do: see a "head and shoulders" pattern and announce the stock will definitely fall to their target. Why it feels right: patterns look compelling on charts. Why it destroys wealth: every technical pattern has a failure rate. It is a probability, not a prophecy. Position sizing must reflect this uncertainty.

Chapter Summary

Technical analysis maps probability landscape — it manages risk, not predicts future.
Candlestick patterns reveal the psychology of buyers and sellers at specific price levels.
Support and resistance are levels where supply/demand imbalances recur — trade from them, not against them.
Volume confirms price; breakouts on high volume are more reliable than on low volume.
200 DMA determines broad trend direction — trade with it, never against it.
RSI below 30 on weekly chart is a high-probability long setup signal in bull markets.
A written trading system with defined entry, stop, target, and position size is non-negotiable.
Your emotions are your worst trading enemy — the system is your discipline made tangible.


Quiz Questions
Q1: A Golden Cross in technical analysis means:

A) Nifty reaches an all-time high
B) 50 DMA crosses above 200 DMA — a bullish trend confirmation
C) Gold price crosses equity returns
D) A stock makes a new 52-week high
Correct: B
Explanation: When the 50-day moving average crosses above the 200-day, it signals the short-term trend has turned bullish relative to the long-term trend — historically a positive medium-term signal.

Q2: A breakout above resistance is more reliable when:

A) Price has been at resistance for only 1 day
B) RSI is above 80
C) Volume is 2–3x the 20-day average
D) The stock has low promoter holding
Correct: C
Explanation: High volume on breakout confirms genuine buying interest from multiple participants. Low-volume breakouts frequently fail (fakeouts) because there are insufficient buyers to sustain the move.

Q3: RSI at 22 on a weekly Nifty chart in March 2020 indicated:

A) The market would definitely crash further
B) Extreme oversold condition — historically associated with strong reversal potential
C) Trading should stop immediately
D) Inflation was rising
Correct: B
Explanation: RSI below 30 on weekly charts at major support levels has historically been a reliable mean-reversion signal in long-term bull markets. Combined with capitulation volume, it was a high-probability long setup.

Q4: Moving a stop loss wider after a trade is entered is dangerous because:

A) It triggers additional brokerage charges
B) It systematically increases loss size and erodes risk management discipline
C) SEBI disallows stop loss modification
D) It confuses the technical analysis signal
Correct: B
Explanation: The pre-defined stop loss reflects maximum acceptable loss. Moving it wider is not patience — it is abandoning risk management under emotional pressure, which eventually causes catastrophic losses.

Q5: Fakeouts in technical analysis are avoided by:

A) Using more indicators simultaneously
B) Waiting for price to close above breakout level for 2 consecutive days
C) Only trading on expiry day
D) Setting tighter stop losses
Correct: B
Explanation: Fakeouts frequently reverse within 1 day. Waiting for 2-day confirmation close above the breakout level filters most false breakouts, though it slightly delays entry on genuine breakouts.
