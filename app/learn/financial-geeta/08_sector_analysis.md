CHAPTER 8: SECTOR & INDUSTRY ANALYSIS
Covering: Sector Rotation · Banking · IT · Pharma · Energy/Commodities · Infrastructure · Consumer Businesses · Government Policy Impact · Emerging Industries · Future Mega Trends

The Hook
Between April 2020 and March 2021, the Nifty Pharma index rose 85%. Between April 2021 and March 2022, it fell 12% while Nifty Metal index rose 67%.
The investors who understood why this happened did not get lucky. They understood the cycle. COVID created massive pharmaceutical demand — generic drug exports, vaccine supply chains, API (Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient) manufacturing. By 2021, COVID tailwinds faded, pricing pressure from US FDA returned, and the sector rotated out.
Meanwhile, China's infrastructure slowdown created global metal demand gaps. India's infrastructure push (Gati Shakti, Smart Cities) added domestic demand. Metal companies' balance sheets had been cleaned up post-2016. The sector was set up for a massive run — and delivered it.
Knowing which sector to be in at which point in the economic cycle is worth more than picking the perfect stock within the wrong sector.
A mediocre banking stock in a banking bull cycle outperforms a great pharma stock in a pharma bear cycle. Sector allocation is the first decision. Stock selection is the second.
Do you know which sector the current economic cycle is favouring — and why?

The Concept
Sector Rotation Theory: As the economy moves through cycles (expansion, peak, contraction, recovery), different sectors outperform. Money flows from sector to sector — and tracking this flow before headlines announce it is the edge.
Economic Cycle and Sector Performance:

Early recovery: Banking (NPAs clear), Auto (pent-up demand), Consumer Discretionary
Mid expansion: IT (corporate tech spend), Industrials, Infrastructure
Late expansion: Energy (demand peak), Commodities, Materials
Contraction: FMCG (defensive), Pharma (healthcare is non-cyclical), Utilities

Banking Sector (Nifty Bank index): Tracks the economic cycle most directly. When GDP grows, credit demand rises, NIM (Net Interest Margin) expands, NPA decreases — banking stocks outperform. Key metrics: NIM, GNPA%, PCR (Provision Coverage Ratio), CASA ratio. HDFC Bank CASA above 46% = structural cost advantage.
IT Sector (Nifty IT index): Driven by global tech spending, particularly US corporate IT budgets. INR depreciation helps (exports in USD, costs in INR). Key metrics: Revenue growth, deal TCV (Total Contract Value), attrition rate. Infosys TCV of $3.5 billion/quarter = strong revenue visibility.
Pharma & Healthcare: Two sub-segments — domestic formulations (insulated from global pressure) and US generics (FDA-regulated, highly competitive, pricing pressure). Post-COVID, domestic hospital chains (Apollo, Narayana) became multi-year growth stories.
Energy and Commodities: Crude oil price is India's biggest macro variable. Every $10 oil price rise adds ₹1.3 lakh crore to India's import bill. Reliance Industries, ONGC, BPCL are direct plays. Rising oil = ONGC gains, BPCL loses margin — the nuance matters.
Infrastructure: Government capex cycles are 5–10 year thematics. India's ₹10 lakh crore infrastructure budget (FY24) created decade-long tailwinds for L&T, Ultratech Cement, Polycab, KEI Industries, and power sector companies.
Consumer Businesses: India's 1.4 billion population + rising per capita income = structural demand story. Rural-urban migration, smartphone penetration, credit access are the drivers. HUL, Marico, Emami, Tata Consumer — all beneficiaries of this 30-year demographic runway.
Government Policy as sector catalyst: Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes worth ₹2 lakh crore across 14 sectors (electronics, pharma, auto, textiles) created direct investment inflows. Dixon Technologies, Amber Enterprises, Tata Electronics — PLI direct beneficiaries.
Emerging industries and future mega trends:

Green Energy: India's 500 GW renewable target by 2030. Adani Green, Tata Power, Waaree Energies — multi-decade theme.
Defence Manufacturing: India's ₹1.72 lakh crore defence budget with Atmanirbhar Bharat focus. HAL, BEL, Bharat Forge — 15-year runway.
Semiconductors: India Semiconductor Mission — TATA Group's ₹91,000 crore fab plant in Gujarat. Ecosystem stocks: ASM Technologies, SPEL Semiconductor.
EV ecosystem: Battery, charging infrastructure, component manufacturers — 10-year transition story.

Financial Application: Krishna says all knowledge flows from one source — and all market movement flows from one source too: economic reality, expressed through sector fundamentals. To truly understand where markets are going, understand where the economy is going. Real Indian Case Study
Nifty Metal Index — The 2020–2022 Super Cycle
In April 2020, Nifty Metal index stood at 1,840 — near a 10-year low. Steel companies were carrying heavy debt. Iron ore prices were subdued. Sentiment was terrible.
By October 2021, Nifty Metal was at 7,574 — a 311% rise in 18 months.
The triggers were visible to sector analysts in mid-2020:

China announced massive infrastructure stimulus ($500 billion+) — direct demand boost for steel, copper, aluminium
India's production of steel at low cost (after debt restructuring of Tata Steel, JSW 2016–2019) = pricing leverage
Global supply chains disrupted, increasing commodity pricing power
India's own infrastructure push beginning to show order intake for steel-intensive projects

Stocks that reflected this: Tata Steel: ₹220 (April 2020) to ₹1,534 (October 2021). SAIL: ₹25 to ₹145. Hindalco: ₹130 to ₹570.
What sector analysis would have shown: Rising PMI in China + India infrastructure capex + global supply disruption = multi-year metal upcycle. The investor who followed sector rotation signals in June-July 2020 entered at 50% discounts to eventual highs.
The lesson: Stocks move within sectors, and sectors move within economic cycles. Know the cycle layer before picking the stock.

The Practical Playbook
Rule 1 — Allocate maximum 25% of portfolio to any single sector.
Sector concentration feels like conviction but creates catastrophic risk if cycle turns. Yes Bank's collapse would not have mattered if banking was capped at 25% of your portfolio.
Rule 2 — Monitor PMI (Purchasing Managers' Index) monthly for sector rotation signals.
PMI above 50 = expansion (bullish for cyclicals: metals, auto, banking). Below 50 = contraction (defensive: FMCG, pharma, utilities). India's PMI is released monthly by S&P Global.
Rule 3 — Track FII (Foreign Institutional Investor) sectoral buying data weekly.
FII buying in a sector signals smart global money is positioning. NSE publishes daily FII/DII data. Sustained 4-week FII buying in a sector = worth investigating.
Rule 4 — Understand government budget allocations for infrastructure and PLI themes.
Union Budget in February announces sectoral capex. Stocks in PLI-beneficiary sectors get re-rated within 3–6 months of announcement. Read the budget summary within 48 hours.
Rule 5 — Build at least one position in a 10-year mega trend sector.
Green energy, defence, semiconductors, EV ecosystem — even a 5% portfolio allocation to a quality company in these sectors could 10x over a decade while your other positions deliver steady returns.

Common Mistakes
Mistake 1 — Buying into a sector after it has already run 100%.
What they do: invest in pharma in October 2020 after it rose 85% — "healthcare is a long-term theme." Why it feels right: the thesis sounds compelling and the sector is in the news. Why it destroys wealth: they are buying distribution, not accumulation. Sector rotation means high-flying sectors correct as money moves to undervalued sectors.
Mistake 2 — Ignoring global commodity cycles for metal and energy stocks.
What they do: analyse Tata Steel's balance sheet in isolation. Why it feels right: company analysis seems sufficient. Why it destroys wealth: metal company profits are almost entirely determined by global steel prices, which are determined by China's demand. Local analysis without global commodity context is incomplete.
Mistake 3 — Treating all IT companies as identical.
What they do: buy any IT company when IT sector is bullish. Why it feels right: rising tide lifts all boats. Why it destroys wealth: large-cap IT (TCS, Infosys) has stable, long-duration deals. Mid-cap IT has shorter deals and US client concentration. When US IT budgets get cut, mid-cap IT falls 50% while large-cap falls 20%.

Chapter Summary

Sector rotation is the flow of money through economic cycles — understanding it precedes stock selection.
Early recovery favours banking and auto; mid-cycle favours IT and industrials; late cycle favours energy.
Banking sector health is measured by NPA trend, NIM, and CASA ratio — not branch count or brand.
IT sector is a USD-earning, INR-cost business — rupee depreciation is structurally positive.
India's infrastructure mega-cycle (₹10 lakh crore annual capex) is a 10-year structural investment theme.
PLI schemes are government-sponsored sector catalysts — budget allocation translates to stock re-rating.
Green energy, defence, and semiconductors are decade-long themes with early-stage entry windows now.
Maximum 25% portfolio concentration in any sector — even the most compelling sector cycle can reverse.


Quiz Questions
Q1: Sector rotation theory suggests that during economic contraction, which sector typically outperforms?

A) Metals and Mining
B) Automotive
C) FMCG and Healthcare (defensive sectors)
D) Infrastructure
Correct: C
Explanation: Defensive sectors (FMCG, pharma, utilities) maintain demand regardless of economic cycle — people still buy soap and medicine in recession. This makes them relative outperformers during contraction.

Q2: Nifty Metal rose 311% between April 2020 and October 2021 primarily because:

A) Indian government privatised all steel companies
B) China's infrastructure stimulus created massive global metal demand alongside Indian supply cost advantages
C) RBI reduced interest rates for metal companies specifically
D) IT sector rotation into metals by retail investors
Correct: B
Explanation: China's $500B+ infrastructure stimulus was the demand catalyst. India's low-cost, restructured steel capacity captured pricing power. This combination created a commodity super-cycle for Indian metal companies.

Q3: PLI (Production Linked Incentive) schemes are important for sector analysis because:

A) They reduce income tax for all manufacturers
B) Government capex allocation directly re-rates stocks in beneficiary sectors within 3–6 months
C) They mandate FII investment in specific sectors
D) SEBI guarantees minimum returns for PLI sector stocks
Correct: B
Explanation: PLI creates direct revenue incentives for domestic manufacturing, improving profit margins and growth visibility. Market re-ratings typically follow within 1–2 quarters of announcement as earnings projections are revised upward.

Q4: For IT sector analysis, INR depreciation against USD is:

A) Negative — imported raw materials cost more
B) Neutral — IT is purely domestic
C) Positive — IT earns revenue in USD and pays costs in INR
D) Decided by RBI on a quarterly basis for IT companies
Correct: C
Explanation: IT companies bill clients in USD. Employees and offices are paid in INR. When USD strengthens vs INR, the same dollar revenue converts to more rupees — directly improving margins and profit.

Q5: Maximum sector allocation of 25% is recommended because:

A) SEBI mandates this for retail investors
B) Sector cycles reverse, and concentration creates catastrophic downside risk
C) 25% is the mutual fund minimum requirement
D) Tax benefits apply only up to 25% sector allocation
Correct: B
Explanation: Yes Bank alone, if it was 40% of your banking allocation and banking was 40% of your portfolio, would have wiped 16% of total wealth. Sector caps limit the damage from any single cycle reversal.
